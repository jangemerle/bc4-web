# Copy Patterns Playbook

Collected headline formulas, reframing techniques, and tone rules from reference sites. Each pattern includes the formula and Kvalt-specific examples.

---

## Headline Formulas

### 1. Provocative Question
**Formula:** "What's after [incumbent]?"
**Source:** Equals
**Why it works:** Creates tension, implies pain without stating it, reader fills in their own frustration.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "What comes after component libraries?"
- "What if your design system had taste?"
- "What's between Tailwind and hiring a design team?"
- "What do you get when a design system has opinions?"

### 2. Triplet Positioning
**Formula:** "[Identity], [capability], [value]."
**Source:** Equals ("first new spreadsheet in 20 years, engineered for AI, designed for trust")
**Why it works:** Three claims in ascending importance. Easy to remember, feels complete.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "The first design system with opinions, engineered for speed, designed for developers who care."
- "One install, full design language, zero meetings required."
- "Components, tokens, motion — decisions included."

### 3. "This isn't X"
**Formula:** "This isn't [misconception]. [Reframe]."
**Source:** Equals ("This isn't just a chatbot. Ask a question. Analyst builds the model.")
**Why it works:** Names the objection before the reader thinks it, then redirects.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "This isn't a component library. It's a design opinion you can install."
- "Not another UI kit. A system that knows why it looks the way it does."
- "Not opinionated — decisive. Every default has a reason. Override any of them."

### 4. "Discovered, not invented"
**Formula:** "[Thing] wasn't invented. It's how [natural truth] has always worked."
**Source:** Equals (about spreadsheets)
**Why it works:** Elevates from product to inevitability. Makes adoption feel natural.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "Design tokens weren't invented. They're how visual consistency has always worked — we just made them composable."
- "Characters aren't themes. They're the personality your product already has — we just gave it a system."

### 5. Implicit Pain
**Formula:** "The first [category] that [implied deficiency of alternatives]."
**Source:** Equals ("first new spreadsheet in 20 years")
**Why it works:** More sophisticated than "Tired of X?" — the criticism is embedded in the claim.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "The first design system that ships with taste."
- "A design system that doesn't assume you'll customize everything yourself."
- "The design system you'd build yourself. Already built."

### 6. Action-Verb-First
**Formula:** "[Verb] [outcome]" not "[Noun] for [audience]"
**Source:** Equals ("Guide your team," "Stand up board-ready reporting," "Delegate your daily questions")
**Why it works:** More energetic, reader-as-agent framing.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "Ship consistent UIs without the meetings."
- "Install a design language, not just components."
- "Override anything — or just ship."

### 7. Self-Deprecating Qualifier
**Formula:** "[Bold claim]. Well, [humorous qualification]. But [redirect to real proof]."
**Source:** Tailwind Showcase ("You can build anything with Tailwind CSS. Well not quite *anything*, like you can't build a spaceship with it. But you can definitely build the website for the spaceship — NASA did.")
**Why it works:** Disarms skepticism by beating the reader to the objection. The humor makes it memorable and the redirect lands on real social proof.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "Your design system for everything. Well, everything that runs in a browser. And maybe a few things that shouldn't."
- "A complete design language in one install. Okay, two installs if you count the peer dependency. But who's counting."
- "The design system you'd build yourself. If you had three months and zero deadlines."

### 8. "Unapologetically [Value]"
**Formula:** "[Product] is unapologetically [quality], and [evidence]."
**Source:** Tailwind ("Tailwind is unapologetically modern, and takes advantage of all the latest and greatest CSS features")
**Why it works:** Positions a potential objection ("too modern/opinionated") as a deliberate strength. The word "unapologetically" signals confidence and filters for the right audience.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "Kvalt is unapologetically opinionated, because good defaults ship faster than endless configuration."
- "Unapologetically designed. Every spacing value, every shadow, every transition — chosen, not computed."

### 9. "By the makers of [Credibility]"
**Formula:** "[Product description], built by the makers of [known thing]."
**Source:** Tailwind Plus ("Beautifully designed, expertly crafted components and templates, built by the makers of Tailwind CSS.")
**Why it works:** Borrows credibility from an established product. Works even at small scale if the "known thing" is respected in the target community.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "Built by [name], the design engineer behind [notable project]."
- "From the mind that ships pixel-perfect at 2 AM."

### 10. "Get everything, forever"
**Formula:** "Get [everything], [forever]. [One-time price]."
**Source:** Tailwind Plus pricing section
**Why it works:** Three anxieties killed in four words: scope ("everything"), duration ("forever"), and model (not subscription). Developers hate subscriptions for tools.

**Kvalt examples:**
- "Every character, every component, every update. Once."
- "Get the full system. Keep it forever. $79."

## Tone Rules (derived from all references)

1. **Never use exclamation marks.** Confidence doesn't shout.
2. **No "amazing", "incredible", "powerful", "revolutionary".** These words mean nothing.
3. **Understated > enthusiastic.** "It works" beats "It's incredible how well it works."
4. **Let testimonials carry explicit pain.** Marketing copy stays aspirational, customer quotes get specific ("saved us 3 months").
5. **Shorter is harder and better.** If it can be said in fewer words, it should be.
6. **Present tense, imperative mood.** "Ship" not "You can ship" not "Enables shipping."
7. **Technical enough to be credible, clear enough to be accessible.** Don't dumb down, don't jargon-wall.

---

## Social Proof Rules

1. **Distribute, don't cluster.** Every page gets at least one testimonial.
2. **Specific metrics > generic praise.** "Reduced build time from 3 days to 4 hours" > "Love this DS!"
3. **Place near purchase anxiety.** Pricing page, getting-started page, comparison sections.
4. **3 recognizable logos > 20 unknown ones.**
5. **Customer quotes carry the pain narrative.** Marketing copy doesn't need to say "tired of inconsistency" if a customer says "we had 14 button variants before Kvalt."

---

## Page Structure Rules

1. **Single CTA per page.** "Get started" or "Install" — not both plus "Watch demo" plus "Join waitlist."
2. **Persona-first navigation.** Let visitors self-select before seeing features.
3. **Named recipes > feature lists.** "The Dashboard" > "Flexible layout components."
4. **FAQ where anxiety peaks.** Pricing page FAQ: adoption time, customization depth, migration path.
5. **Launch banner above nav.** Slim persistent bar announcing the latest release.
6. **Architecture diagram.** Tokens > Components > Characters > Your App — makes the system feel engineered.
