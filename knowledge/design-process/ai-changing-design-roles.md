# AI Is Fundamentally Changing Design Roles

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — Jenny Wen (Anthropic, ex-Figma)
**URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eh8bcBIAAFo
**Added:** 2026-03-17
**Type:** Podcast interview (~1h)

---

## Who is Jenny Wen

Head of design for Claude Co-work at Anthropic. Previously director of design at Figma (led FigJam and Slides), designer at Dropbox, Square, and Shopify. She's operating at the bleeding edge of how AI reshapes design work — literally building the tools that are changing the profession.

---

## Key Takeaways

### 1. The traditional design process is dead

The sequential process designers were taught (research → wireframe → mockup → handoff) no longer holds. Mockup time dropped from 60–70% of the role to 30–40%. The rest is spent helping engineers execute, making decisions, and steering product direction.

### 2. Vision timelines compressed dramatically

Teams used to create 2–5 year (even 10-year) vision decks. Now it's 3–6 month prototypes that "point people in the right direction." The deliverable shifted from polished decks to working prototypes.

### 3. Engineers can't keep up with themselves

It's not just designers adapting. Engineering teams run 7+ AI agents constantly. Even engineers struggle to track what their own agents are producing. The entire product development cycle is accelerating for everyone.

### 4. Three designer archetypes for the AI era

Jenny identifies three types of designers she finds most valuable now:

- **Systems thinkers** — people who can design frameworks and patterns that scale, not just individual screens. They think in components, tokens, and rules.
- **Prototypers / builders** — designers who can code or use AI tools to produce working prototypes quickly, blurring the line between design and engineering.
- **Strategic decision-makers** — people who can decide what gets built and why. Taste and judgment matter, but the real value is accountability for decisions.

### 5. Taste alone isn't enough anymore

AI (Claude Code specifically mentioned) is already helping people come up with ideas and generate designs. Designers who rely solely on "good taste" as their differentiator may be overvaluing that skill. The moat is in understanding users, making tradeoffs, and shipping.

### 6. Designers need to "let engineers cook"

Instead of blocking engineering with pixel-perfect handoffs, designers should provide direction and let engineers (with AI assistance) iterate. The handoff model is becoming more collaborative and less sequential.

### 7. Co-work as a design introspection tool

Jenny uses Claude Co-work to analyze her own notes (1:1s, memos, interview notes) and extract patterns she wouldn't see herself. Example: she had it read all her past notes and generate a rubric for how she evaluates design craft in interviews — surfacing implicit criteria she hadn't articulated.

### 8. The role is expanding, not shrinking

Design isn't dying — it's expanding into territory that was previously PM or engineering. Designers who adapt are becoming more central to product decisions, not less. The ones who struggle are those clinging to mockup-centric workflows.

## Relevance to Kvalt

**Product positioning:** Kvalt's target users are exactly the designers Jenny describes — people who need to move fast, can't spend 70% of their time mocking up, and need a system that maintains quality at speed. The DS should market itself as an accelerator for this new workflow, not just a component library.

**Design system as infrastructure:** The "systems thinker" archetype is who builds and maintains design systems. Kvalt should speak to these people — they value tokens, consistency, and scalable patterns over flashy demos.

**Prototype-first workflow:** If designers are prototyping more and mocking less, the DS needs excellent developer ergonomics. Quick setup, clear APIs, good defaults. The Figma-to-code pipeline matters more than ever.

**AI-assisted design:** Jenny's note about using AI to analyze her own notes validates the knowledge base approach we're building here. The DS documentation could also lean into AI-readability — structured tokens, clear naming, machine-parseable conventions.
